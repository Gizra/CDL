WHAT IS THE KABBALAH?

Literally meaning "tradition" or "reception", Kabbalah is the traditional term for the medieval esoteric teachings of Judaism and for Jewish mysticism. Although it has much earlier roots, the term Kabbalah  refers usually to the discipline which developed in Provence and  Cataluña from  the second half of the 12th century.

 Following the Jewish expulsion from Spain in 1492, a new trend of the Kabbalah, the Lurianic Kabbalah, developed in Safed,  a town in 16th-century Ottoman Palestine, called by the Jews The Land of Israel.

  • THE ORIGINS OF JEWISH MYSTICISM

    The roots of the Kabbalah go back to early esoteric Jewish literature such as the Heavenly Sanctuaries Literature (Hekhalot literature), Work of the Chariot (Ma'aseh Merkava), The Book of Creation (Sefer Yetzira); this literature itself originates in earlier sources going back to the 1st-2ed century CE.

  • THE BOOK OF SPLENDOR – SEFER HA-ZOHAR

    The book of the Zohar, the central opus of the Kabbalah, was composed by Rabbi Moshe de Leon in Guadalajara between 1280 - 1286, but is believed by some scholars to have been written by a circle of authors.

    The book purports to have been written by Rabbi Shimeon Bar Yohai, a Jewish sage of the second century, who while hiding from the Roman persecutions in a cave for thirteen years studied the Torah, inspired by the prophet.

    The Zohar is a mystical commentary on the Torah (Pentateuch).

    It revolves round two axes: one - the mysteries of the emanations of God, called the Sefirot, which are reflected in many symbols in the created world; the other - the situation of the Jew and his fate in this world and in the world of souls. The Zohar's major interest was a creation of an alternative reality where redemption replaces exile. Human action is thus seen as a vital contribution in the cosmic struggle between exile and redemption.

  • FROM SPAIN TO SAFED : THE LURIANIC KABBALAH

    Following the national trauma of the expulsion from Spain in 1492, Jews began to search for signs indicating the arrival of the awaited Jewish Messiah. In the 1500s the community of Safed, in the Galilee (the northern part of the Land of Israel), became the center of Jewish mystical, exegetical, legal and liturgical developments. 

  • THE NEW CENTER IN SAFED

    The school of Safed started with R. Joseph Caro, R. Shlomo Alkabetz, and R. Moshe Cordovero between 1535 and 1572, and culminated with Isaac Luria, its most influential leader.  While earlier Kabbalah centered on the return of man to the wellspring of his life through the contemplation of the upper worlds, the Safed mystics combined the Kabbalistic doctrine and practice with Messianic and apocalyptic trends.

  • THE SPREAD OF THE KABBALAH OF SAFED

    Later on Safed Kabbalah spread to Egypt, Turkey and Italy, and then to Jewish centers all over the Jewish Diaspora. 

    In the 17th and 18th centuries Safed Kabbalah and Sefer ha-Zohar generated the messianic movement known as Sabbateanism. In Eastern Europe it stimulated a special movement called the Hasidut.

  • AN ESOTERIC DOCTRINE?

    The Kabbalah in general, and especially the Zohar, was considered by many a doctrine which can be correctly understood only by scholars who are well versed in the Jewish sources. After Sabbateanism, Kabbalah's delving into basic questions regarding the nature of the divine caused it to be conceived as a dangerous doctrine which may lead simple believers astray. It was thus usually forbidden to people below the age of forty and to those who were not learned.

    The expansion of Kabbalah was much assisted by the invention of print, overcoming the restrictions which had been imposed on its dissemination and study.
