KAFKA AND JUDAISM

Although from a Jewish family, Kafka's Jewish education was minimal and amounted to a few visits a year at the Synagogue. Yet, in 1911, having been enchanted by a Yiddish theatre performance, he took up the study of Yiddish language and literature; as a consequence of this his interest in Judaism was aroused. During the early 1920's in Prague Kafka started studying modern Hebrew. Under the influence of his last lover, Dora Diamant, who came from an Orthodox Jewish family, Kafka even showed interest in the Talmud! It was during that time, when they were living in Berlin, that Kafka took classes in Judaism.

  • KAFKA'S HEBREW BOOKLET

    Beginning in 1917, Kafka spent time learning Hebrew, first by himself, and later with various teachers. This is one out of five booklets filled with vocabulary by Kafka and the only one kept at the National Library of Israel.

    In his last years he studied with Pu'ah Ben Tovim, a young student from Jerusalem, who came recommended by his good friend S.H. Bergmann, and was staying as a student in Bergmann's mother home in Prague. Kafka and Ben-Tovim  were reading the Hebrew novel Breakdown and Bereavement by Y.H. Brenner, and there are testimonies that he was able to speak and even to write simple letters in Hebrew.

    Some of the words included in this booklet very much remind us of the imaginary world of the author: passion, cockroach, escape, illusion etc.

  • KAFKA AND ZIONISM

    Although initially Kafka viewed himself as a Socialist and rejected the Zionist views of his childhood friend S.H. Bergmann, he deliberated several times, with four of his women partners to immigrate to Palestine! A short while before his death he wrote to Bergmann, who had been in Palestine since 1920, of his plan to visit. The plan never materialized as Kafka's illness deteriorated rapidly.

     'Kafka's lifelong dream was to make aliyah'
  • HOSTING KAFKA AT BERGMANN'S HOME

    In summer 1923 Kafka had written to Bergmann about his plan to visit Palestine. In a letter dated August 1923 Bergmann wrote  to his wife Else the following:

    "Regarding Kafka: I would of course be very glad to have him here, but I beg you not to take this burden upon yourself and upon our house. The room in the cellar is taken up by Lea (the maid), and it is doubtful whether this room is suited for living, especially when it comes to Franz. Even Lea had not wanted this room…As long as mother is here, we do not have a spare room. Even if she leaves – who knows when that will happen – there will still be the problem of attending to Franz. It is one thing to help him as much as possible when he is living abroad, but it's another, when you invite him to our home and take upon yourself the responsibility and the care. This will end up at the expense of the children! … You know that I love Franz very much, and would like him to stay with me, but I ask you to make no commitments or to build up (his) hopes."
